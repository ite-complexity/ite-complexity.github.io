About

I develop mathematical frameworks at the intersection of theoretical physics, information geometry, and deep learning theory. My work uses deep neural networks as controlled systems to study universal principles of model formation — with particular focus on quantifying data complexity, model hierarchies, and the emergence of theoretical understanding. Drawing on methods from statistical physics, information theory, Morse theory, and gauge theory, I aim to understand the limits of learning systems and the foundations of modeling beyond classical statistics.

My doctoral thesis, Quantifying Theory Formation through Information-Geometric Approaches to Deep Learning, develops novel connections between statistical physics and deep learning phenomenology — characterizing phase transitions in regularized neural networks, quantifying data complexity using topological methods, and bridging physics concepts with AI theory.

My path to this research was non-linear: I began in information geometry and cosmology, specialized in advanced quantum field theory and L∞-algebras at Heidelberg and Berlin, and published on homological approaches to AdS/CFT — before independently transitioning to learning theory. This cross-disciplinary background, spanning high-energy physics to complexity science, allows me to identify unconventional connections and bring novel perspectives to fundamental problems in learning and theory formation.
